PART 1 — MELBOURNE BIOBLITZ 2014

A single day, hundreds of species

In November 2014, hundreds of citizen scientists took part in Melbourne's annual BioBlitz — a 24-hour sprint to record as many species as possible across the city. The result: 670 sightings spanning birds, plants, insects, mammals and more. The map below plots every sighting geographically. Click a group in the legend to isolate it.

📍 Each dot = one wildlife sighting. Click taxa in the legend to filter.

Source: Atlas of Living Australia / Participate.

Most Sighted Species

The Magpie-lark topped the list with 17 sightings — a testament to how well this species has adapted to urban parks and gardens. The Common Myna and Silver Gull followed closely. The top 10 is dominated entirely by birds, reflecting their abundance and ease of identification by citizen scientists.

Top 10 most sighted species across all taxa. Source: Melbourne BioBlitz 2014.

Breakdown by Animal Group

Plants make up 46% of all sightings — the largest single group — followed by birds at 42%. Together they account for nearly 9 in 10 sightings. Insects, mammals and reptiles were far less commonly recorded, likely due to their smaller size or more secretive behaviour rather than true absence. Click a segment to highlight it.

Proportional breakdown of BioBlitz sightings by taxa. Source: Melbourne BioBlitz 2014.

PART 2 — TIMING & REPORTING

When do they appear, and how are they recorded?

The BioBlitz ran across several weeks of submission windows, but the bulk of records concentrated on the official event day. The heatmap reveals which animal groups used which reporting channels — showing how technology shaped what was recorded.

Sightings Over Time

The spike on 15 November 2014 — the official BioBlitz event day — dwarfs all other dates, with over 200 sightings in a single day. The smaller peaks before and after represent early submissions and late entries, reflecting the enthusiastic participation of Melbourne's community naturalists. The red dashed line marks the peak sightings day.

Daily sighting count across the BioBlitz period. Source: Melbourne BioBlitz 2014.

How Were They Reported?

Most records came via the Participate app and handwritten citizen expert forms. Birds dominated digital reporting via Instagram and Twitter, while plants were predominantly recorded by hand — suggesting that plant identification still relies heavily on expert knowledge. Darker green = more sightings via that channel.

Taxa vs reporting method heatmap. Source: Melbourne BioBlitz 2014.

PART 3 — CANBERRA (ACT)

Wildlife incidents in the nation's capital

Unlike Melbourne's voluntary sightings, Canberra's dataset captures emergency wildlife incidents — animals that required intervention by ACT wildlife services. Nearly 5,725 incidents were logged between 2016 and 2026. Use the dropdown on the map to filter by species.

Incident Locations Across the ACT

📍 Every dot = one recorded wildlife incident. Use the dropdown to filter by species.

Source: ACT Wildlife Incident Data, 2016–2026.

Which Species Appear Most?

Eastern Grey Kangaroos account for over 2,000 incidents — more than the next three species combined. As Canberra's suburbs expand into kangaroo habitat, encounters are inevitable. Wombats and brushtail possums follow closely. The presence of foxes (an introduced pest) highlights the ongoing challenge of managing invasive species alongside native wildlife. Click the legend to highlight a species.

Top 10 species by total incident count. Source: ACT Wildlife Incidents.

A Decade of Incidents

Incident counts dipped sharply in 2020–21, likely reflecting reduced reporting during COVID-19 lockdowns rather than a genuine wildlife decline. Since 2022, numbers have surged to record highs — 2023 recorded 834 incidents, the highest single year in the dataset. This upward trend suggests that as Canberra grows outward, human-wildlife encounters are becoming more frequent, not less.

Annual ACT wildlife incident counts, 2016–2026. Note: 2026 is partial (Jan–May only).

PART 4 — COMPARISON

Melbourne vs Canberra: two cities, two stories

Melbourne and Canberra capture wildlife in very different ways — one through a community celebration of biodiversity, the other through emergency intervention records. Comparing the two reveals the dramatically different wildlife landscapes of Australia's two most studied cities in this dataset.

Animal Groups Side by Side

Canberra's mammal count of 5,213 towers over Melbourne's 24 sightings — not because Melbourne has fewer mammals, but because the datasets measure different things. Birds appear at similar rates in both cities (~311–312), suggesting consistent urban bird populations across south-eastern Australia.

Note: Melbourne = voluntary sightings (BioBlitz, one day). Canberra = emergency incidents (10 years). Direct comparison should account for these methodological differences.

PART 5 — GEOGRAPHIC COVERAGE

Where does our data come from?

Australia spans nearly 7.7 million square kilometres, yet detailed urban wildlife data remains concentrated in just a handful of cities. This choropleth shows the geographic coverage of the two datasets — highlighting both what we know, and the vast gaps that remain across the continent.

Wildlife Records by State

Dark green = ACT (5,725 incidents). Mid green = Victoria (670 sightings). Grey = no data. Source: ACT Government Wildlife Services & Atlas of Living Australia. Australian State Boundaries — ABS ASGS Edition 3 (GDA2020), simplified via Mapshaper.

🌿 What this tells us

The concentration of data in just two states reflects a broader challenge in urban ecology: systematic wildlife monitoring in Australia is still in its early stages. Western Australia, Queensland, and the Northern Territory — which together cover over 60% of the continent — have no comparable urban wildlife databases publicly available. Expanding citizen science programs like BioBlitz and standardised wildlife reporting to other cities could dramatically improve our understanding of how native species are adapting to Australia's rapidly urbanising landscape.
