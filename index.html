Wildlife in Australian Cities

Kangaroos crossing suburban streets. Wombats in backyards. A possum in the ceiling. Australia's cities are shared with thousands of native species. This visualisation explores nearly 6,000 wildlife incidents recorded in Canberra and 670 species sightings from a single day in Melbourne — to understand where, when, and which animals live alongside us.

1 Part 1 — Melbourne BioBlitz 2014

A single day, hundreds of species

In November 2014, hundreds of citizen scientists took part in Melbourne's annual BioBlitz — a 24-hour sprint to record as many species as possible. The result: 670 sightings spanning birds, plants, insects, mammals and more. Click a group in the legend to isolate it.

📍 Each dot = one wildlife sighting. Source: Atlas of Living Australia / Participate.

Most Sighted Species

The Magpie-lark topped the list with 17 sightings — a testament to how well it has adapted to urban parks. The top 10 is dominated entirely by birds, reflecting their abundance and ease of identification by citizen scientists.

Source: Melbourne BioBlitz 2014.

Breakdown by Animal Group

Plants make up 46% of all sightings, followed by birds at 42%. Together they account for nearly 9 in 10 sightings. Insects, mammals and reptiles were far less commonly recorded, likely due to their smaller size. Click a segment to highlight it.

Source: Melbourne BioBlitz 2014.

2 Part 2 — Timing & Reporting

When do they appear, and how are they recorded?

The BioBlitz ran across several weeks, but the bulk of records concentrated on the official event day. The charts below reveal when sightings peaked and which reporting channels different animal groups used.

Sightings Over Time

The spike on 15 November 2014 — the official BioBlitz event day — dwarfs all other dates, with over 200 sightings in a single day. The red dashed line marks the peak.

How Were They Reported?

Most records came via the Participate app and handwritten citizen expert forms. Plants were predominantly recorded by hand — suggesting plant identification still relies heavily on expert knowledge. Darker green = more sightings.

3 Part 3 — Canberra (ACT)

Wildlife incidents in the nation's capital

Unlike Melbourne's voluntary sightings, Canberra's dataset captures emergency wildlife incidents — animals requiring intervention by ACT wildlife services. Nearly 5,725 incidents were logged between 2016 and 2026. Use the dropdown on the map to filter by species.

📍 Every dot = one recorded wildlife incident. Source: ACT Wildlife Incident Data, 2016–2026.

Which Species Appear Most?

Eastern Grey Kangaroos account for over 2,000 incidents — more than the next three species combined. The presence of foxes (an introduced pest) highlights the challenge of managing invasive species alongside native wildlife. Click the legend to highlight.

Source: ACT Wildlife Incidents.

A Decade of Incidents

Counts dipped in 2020–21 during COVID-19 lockdowns, then surged to record highs. 2023 recorded 834 incidents — the highest single year in the dataset, suggesting encounters are becoming more frequent.

Source: ACT Wildlife Incidents. 2026 is partial (Jan–May only).

4 Part 4 — Comparison

Melbourne vs Canberra: two cities, two stories

Canberra's mammal count of 5,213 towers over Melbourne's 24 sightings — not because Melbourne has fewer mammals, but because the datasets measure different things. Birds appear at similar rates (~311–312) in both cities, suggesting consistent urban bird populations across south-eastern Australia.

Melbourne = voluntary sightings (BioBlitz, one day). Canberra = emergency incidents (10 years).

5 Part 5 — Geographic Coverage

Where does our data come from?

Australia spans nearly 7.7 million square kilometres, yet detailed urban wildlife data remains concentrated in just a handful of cities. This choropleth shows the geographic coverage of both datasets — and highlights the vast gaps that remain.

Dark green = ACT (5,725 incidents). Mid green = Victoria (670 sightings). Grey = no data. Source: ACT Government Wildlife Services & Atlas of Living Australia. Boundaries: ABS ASGS Edition 3 (GDA2020), simplified via Mapshaper.

The concentration of data in just two states reflects a broader challenge in urban ecology: systematic wildlife monitoring in Australian cities is still in its early stages. Western Australia, Queensland, and the Northern Territory — covering over 60% of the continent — have no comparable urban wildlife databases publicly available. Expanding citizen science programs like BioBlitz to other cities could dramatically improve our understanding of how native species are adapting to urbanisation.
